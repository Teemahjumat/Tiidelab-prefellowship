Ahmed Hoosen Deedat

He was born on the 1st of July, 1918 and died on the 8th of August, 2005. He was a South African writer and a public speaker of Indian descent. He was best known as a Muslim missionary, who held numerous inter-religious public debates with evangelical Christians, as well as video lectures on Islam, Christianity, and the Bible.

Deedat established the IPCI , an international Islamic missionary organisation, and wrote several widely distributed booklets on Islam and Christianity. He was awarded the King Faisal International Prize in 1986 for his fifty years of missionary work. He wrote and lectured in English.


Early years 1918-1942

Deedat was born in the town of Tadkeshwar,British India in 1918. His father had emigrated to South Africa and Deedat left India to join his father at the age of 9, he lost his mother few months later. Deedat was diligent to his studies, overcame language barrier and excelled in school until he completed standard 6 and had to quit school due to financial circumstances.

Writings and Speeches

Deedat published and mass-produced over one dozen palm-sized booklets focusing on the following major themes.

  • Is the Bible God's Word?
  • What The Bible Says About Muhammad.
  • Combat Kit Against Bible Thumpers.

These are just a few listed amongst his numerous publishes.


Criticisms

It was emerged that his dawah centre, IPCI was financed by the Bin Laden family and that he had met Osama Bin Laden, whom he described positively. Deedat's debates and writings have been labelled as a form of apologetics by Lloyd V. J. Ridgeon, professor of Islamic Studies at the University of Glasgow. Muslim scholar Farid Esack has criticised Deedat, comparing him to such fundamentalists as Rabbi Meir Kahane and Jerry Falwell, and writing:

Deedat's multitude of anti-Christian, anti-Jewish and anti-Hindu videotapes have told us all that there is to be told about the other, and we are comfortable with that. There are times, of course, when questions surface about the importance of correct dogma, about the importance of labels to a God whom we believe sees beyond labels and looks at the hearts of people. Instead of pursuing these questions, we hasten back and seek refuge in "the known." We order another of those Deedat tapes.
